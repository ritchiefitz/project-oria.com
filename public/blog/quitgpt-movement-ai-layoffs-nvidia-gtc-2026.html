#QuitGPT Erupts, AI Replaces 34,000 Jobs, and Nvidia's GTC Looms Large

The #QuitGPT movement explodes after OpenAI's Pentagon deal, Block and Oracle slash thousands of jobs for AI, and Nvidia's GTC 2026 promises to be the "Super Bowl of AI."

This week delivered one of the most turbulent stretches the AI industry has seen. A consumer revolt against OpenAI went viral, tens of thousands of workers learned their jobs are being replaced by AI systems, and the entire tech world is holding its breath for what Nvidia will unveil at its annual GPU Technology Conference. Here's everything you need to know.

The #QuitGPT Movement Explodes

When OpenAI announced a deal to deploy its models on Pentagon military systems, the backlash was swift and massive. The #QuitGPT hashtag became the top trending topic across social platforms as 2.5 million people cancelled their ChatGPT subscriptions or publicly pledged to boycott the company.

The numbers are staggering. ChatGPT app uninstalls in the United States surged 295% day-over-day, and one-star reviews on app stores jumped 775%. On March 3, protesters gathered outside OpenAI's San Francisco headquarters, marking one of the first large-scale physical demonstrations against an AI company.

The fallout has reshuffled the competitive landscape. Anthropic's Claude has climbed to the #1 position in model rankings, gaining a wave of users fleeing OpenAI. The irony is sharp: OpenAI is losing consumer trust while gaining military contracts, and Anthropic is gaining users while reportedly losing some government clients. The market is effectively splitting along ethical lines, and users are voting with their wallets.

"This is the first time we've seen a consumer revolt against an AI company at this scale. It signals that the public is paying attention to how AI gets deployed, not just how well it performs."

AI Replaces 34,000 Workers in a Single Week

While the #QuitGPT movement dominated social media, a quieter but arguably more consequential story unfolded in corporate boardrooms. Two major companies announced massive workforce reductions explicitly tied to AI capabilities.

Block (the parent company of Square, Cash App, and Afterpay) cut 4,000 employees—nearly 40% of its team. CEO Jack Dorsey was blunt about the reason: these roles are "redundant compared to cheaper and more efficient AI tools." No euphemisms about restructuring or strategic realignment. Just a direct admission that AI can now do what thousands of humans were doing.

Oracle went even further, planning to lay off 20,000 to 30,000 employees to generate $8–10 billion in savings that will be funneled directly into AI infrastructure. The cuts span multiple divisions and began rolling out this month.

Together, these two announcements alone account for up to 34,000 jobs eliminated in a single week. And these aren't isolated cases—they represent a broader pattern of companies converting human labor budgets into AI compute budgets. The message from the C-suite is becoming unmistakable: if your job can be described as a process, AI is coming for it.

The Human Cost

What makes this wave different from previous tech layoffs is the explicitness. Previous rounds of job cuts were dressed up as "efficiency improvements" or "strategic pivots." Now, executives are openly naming AI as the replacement. This transparency is jarring but arguably more honest—and it forces a conversation about how society adapts when the replacement isn't hypothetical anymore.

Nvidia GTC 2026: The "Super Bowl of AI"

As the dust settles from this week's upheaval, all eyes are turning to Nvidia's annual GPU Technology Conference (GTC), running March 16–19. Wall Street firm Truist has called it the "Super Bowl of AI," and expectations are enormous.

Analysts widely expect Nvidia to unveil new hardware optimized for inference workloads—a signal that the industry is shifting from training massive models to deploying them at scale. The company's stock has been volatile ahead of the event, closing Friday at $180.25 as traders position themselves for potential announcements.

The stakes extend beyond Nvidia itself. Morgan Stanley published a sweeping new report this week warning that a transformative leap in artificial intelligence is imminent, driven by an unprecedented accumulation of compute at America's top AI labs. Executives at major labs are reportedly telling investors to brace for progress that will "shock" them.

But the intelligence explosion comes with a brutal infrastructure constraint. Morgan Stanley's "Intelligence Factory" model projects a net U.S. power shortfall of 9 to 18 gigawatts through 2028—a gap that could bottleneck the entire AI buildout if not addressed rapidly.

Other Notable Developments

  • OpenAI acquired Promptfoo, the open-source AI testing framework. The tool will remain open source and continue serving its existing users.
  • Apple unveiled the MacBook Neo at $599—the cheapest Mac ever—positioning it as a mass-market AI device powered by the A18 Pro chip.
  • AI legislation accelerated across the U.S., with Washington and Utah passing major AI bills and Virginia pushing through three new measures.
  • DeepSeek is preparing V4, a multimodal model optimized for Chinese chips (Huawei Ascend and Cambricon), marking its first major release since R1.
  • NASA's Perseverance rover completed the first Mars drives ever planned by AI, using Anthropic's Claude vision-language models to autonomously generate safe waypoints.

What This Means

This week crystallized a tension that has been building for months. On one side, AI capabilities are advancing at a pace that genuinely justifies the word "transformative"—models scoring at expert level on professional benchmarks, autonomous systems driving rovers on Mars, companies replacing entire departments with AI workflows. On the other side, the human consequences are becoming impossible to ignore: mass layoffs named explicitly as AI-driven, a consumer revolt over military AI deployment, and infrastructure constraints that threaten to bottleneck everything.

The next week will bring Nvidia's GTC announcements, which could set the tone for the rest of 2026. But regardless of what Jensen Huang unveils on stage, the broader trajectory is clear: AI is no longer an emerging technology waiting for its moment. The moment is now, and the world is scrambling to keep up.
